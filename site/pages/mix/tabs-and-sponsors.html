Look for a natural clearing with good drainage, slight elevation, and protection from prevailing winds. Avoid low ground where moisture gathers and ridges where wind scours the structure. Local materials adjust better to the climate and last longer when allowed to dry before use. Cabins endure best when built low and broad, with steep roofs to shed rain and deep eaves to protect walls from moisture. Keep the structure simple and purposeful. Stone footings lift the cabin just enough to prevent rot, while multi-use beams and posts strengthen the frame without unnecessary complexity. A cabin that flexes slightly with seasonal change will survive far longer than one built rigid and overworked. - title: Living main_column: >- ### Living With the Forest, Not Against It A good cabin blends into its surroundings rather than clearing them away. Leave trees and undergrowth where possible to block wind and prevent erosion, especially on the colder sides of the structure. The forest itself provides insulation, shade, and protection when respected. Plan for smoke, scent, and movement. Position the hearth so smoke disperses into open canopy, store food securely above ground, and place work areas close to reduce daily disturbance.
